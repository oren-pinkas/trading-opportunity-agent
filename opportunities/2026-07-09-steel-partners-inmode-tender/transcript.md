## Round 1 — Independent Research (parallel, blind to each other)

### Bull (Catalyst-hunter, sonnet)

Textbook competing-bid dynamic. Steel Partners bid $16.75/share topping the CEO-led buyout group's $16.20/share (+$0.55, +3.4% premium). Board decision pending. Bull thesis: this is a process thesis, not a fundamentals thesis — bidding contests rarely stay static; once a board opens itself to a higher outside cash bid it has a fiduciary obligation to evaluate it as a possible "Superior Proposal," and the incumbent CEO-led group frequently has a matching right and may bump rather than lose the deal. Either the board accepts the higher bid, or the CEO group bumps to defend — either way the headline cash number rises. Proposed: long INMD common, enter now while the process is live, exit on the board's public determination (since once a definitive agreement is signed at a fixed cash price, any residual spread is just time-value-to-close, not a catalyst play). Explicitly flagged the price feed ($118.84 → $169.23 → $237.81) as economically incoherent with a $16.75 cash tender and stated the thesis rests entirely on deal-process mechanics, not on the reported price action — while conceding this caps confidence in any quantitative entry/exit timing derived from the feed.

### Bear (Skeptic, sonnet)

This is a live bidding war, not a completed deal — the board decision is explicitly "pending," so everything past that point is a probability distribution, not a fact. The $0.55/share (3.4%) bump is thin for an outside bidder to force through against an entrenched CEO-led group with board relationships and possibly rollover equity commitments. Specific risks: (1) board rejects both bids or simply reaffirms the $16.20 deal, especially if Steel Partners' offer lacks committed financing detail; (2) bid escalation/go-shop/match-right periods stall the process for months; (3) Steel Partners' financing is undisclosed and unconfirmed — a classic pattern preceding withdrawn or reduced bids; (4) the bid could simply be withdrawn; (5) regulatory/antitrust risk is unaddressed in the dossier; (6) the base rate for contested M&A is NOT always "higher bid wins cleanly" — reaffirming the original deal or a stalled process are both plausible outcomes the bull case ignores. Also flagged the price feed's +100% cumulative move against a $16.75 cash ceiling as disqualifying for any quantitative edge claim: we cannot establish what the market is currently paying for this outcome, which is the single most important number for a merger-arb trade. Position: skeptical / no-trade-or-minimal-size pending board determination, confirmed financing, and a sane price series.

## Round 2 — Rebuttal (parallel, each shown the other two's Round 1 positions)

### Bull rebuttal

Disagrees with bear's "coin flip" framing: once a board is faced with a competing all-cash bid at a premium to a signed deal, especially one involving CEO-group rollover equity, Revlon-type fiduciary duties make it hard to wave through the lower, insider-conflicted bid absent a demonstrably superior non-price term on the incumbent side — this cuts against bear's skepticism, not for it. Flags that neither bear nor quant addressed downside shape: the realistic break scenario isn't reversion to an unknown undisturbed price, it's reversion to the *existing, still-live* $16.20 signed deal — a materially shallower left tail than a typical single-bidder arb (Quant's own suggestion of protective puts near $16.20 implicitly concedes this same floor). Concedes quant's base rate (0.80-0.88 close, ~30% bump chance) validates the *direction* of the bull thesis but undercuts the *magnitude* — the blended terminal (~$17.02) is only ~1.6% above the current $16.75 bid, thinner upside than bull's Round 1 framing implied. Would change bull's mind: board reaffirms $16.20 without running a real process against Steel; disclosure that Steel's bid is financing-contingent/non-binding; a stalled process beyond 60-90 days; any antitrust referral; or clean data showing the market spread already sub-1%. Explicitly CONCEDES the gating conditions: "I cannot defend entering size against a feed that shows INMD at $118.84→$169.23→$237.81 against a $16.75 cash tender — that's not a modeling nuance, it's a data-integrity failure." Moves from "enter now" to "verified-small-or-no-trade."

### Bear rebuttal

Attacks quant's P(close) ≈ 0.80-0.88 directly: that number borrows the base rate for "2 FUNDED bidders," but Steel Partners' financing is not confirmed anywhere in the record — only an announced offer. The correct reference class is "unconfirmed topping bid vs. incumbent signed deal," which has a materially worse historical hit rate; suggests haircutting to 0.55-0.70. Identifies the single biggest underweighted risk as *process/time risk as an independent loss scenario* — a board can sit on competing bids for months, run a go-shop that resets the clock, face litigation from either side, or simply take the lower, more certain deal; none of this requires antitrust or macro shocks, and it is the most common reason "higher bid" theses in merger-arb underperform naive EV. Argues quant's own gating language ("cannot observe real spread, cannot size break tail, will not act on the feed, requires ≥3-5% real spread before sizing") is substantively identical to bear's own no-trade position, just relabeled "verify-then-maybe" — and that quant's pre-commitment to a position size (≤1.5%) and a headline 80-88% number risks anchoring the room before the gating conditions are even met. Would flip to willing-to-trade given: (1) a clean, coherent price series enabling a real spread-to-offer calculation; (2) public confirmation of Steel Partners' financing (debt commitment letter or equivalent); (3) evidence the board has actually engaged Steel Partners (diligence access, advisors retained) rather than merely disclosing receipt of an unsolicited offer; (4) a visible real spread ≥3% to $16.75, signaling the market is pricing real completion/timing risk with favorable asymmetry.

TO SIZED TRADE (need all three): (1) a sane, corroborated real-time price feed; (2) real gross spread ≥4-5% to $16.75 AND trading at/above the $16.20 floor; (3) on-record confirmation Steel's bid is a bona fide submitted offer, not an indication of interest.
